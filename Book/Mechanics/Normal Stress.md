#mechanics

## Definition
> [!definition]
> **Normal stress** ($\sigma$) is the [[internal force]] per unit area acting *perpendicular* to the face it acts on. It is the parent category that encompasses [[Axial Stress]] (uniform, along a member's long axis) and [[Bending Stress]] (varying linearly across a cross-section).

Notes:
- "Normal" is a geometry term — it means *perpendicular*. A normal stress is always oriented at 90° to the face it acts on, whether tensile (pulling) or compressive (pushing).
- The distinction that matters in practice: **axial stress** is uniform across a cross-section; **bending stress** varies linearly, reaching a maximum at the outer fibers and passing through zero at the [[Neutral Axis]]. Both are normal stresses.
- Contrast with [[Shear Stress]], which acts parallel to the face. Real cross-sections under combined loading often have both normal and shear stress acting simultaneously — see [[Combined Loading]].

## Examples and Non-Examples

- **Axial tension.** A rod pulled along its length develops a uniform tensile normal stress $\sigma = F/A$ across every cross-section. The stress vector points away from the cut face — textbook [[Axial Stress]].

- **Bending fiber stress.** The bottom fiber of a simply supported beam loaded at mid-span is in tension; the top fiber is in compression. Both are normal stresses (perpendicular to the cross-section face), but the value varies from $+\sigma_{max}$ to $-\sigma_{max}$ across the depth — this is [[Bending Stress]].

- **Counter-example — shear stress is not normal stress.** A bolt loaded transversely has stress *parallel* to its cross-section face. That is [[Shear Stress]], not normal stress, even though the bolt "looks like" an axially loaded rod from the outside.
