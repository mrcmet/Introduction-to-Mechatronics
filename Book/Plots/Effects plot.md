# **Definition**

---

> [!Definition]
> An effects plot is a graph used to show how changes in an independent variable affect a measured dependent variable. The horizontal axis represents a real, physical domain such as time, position, temperature, or voltage, while the vertical axis represents a measured response. Effects plots are used to explore trends, correlations, sensitivities, and variability in data, without assuming a known or exact mathematical relationship between the variables.
  
---

## **Key Ideas**

- The x-axis represents a real physical [[Domains |domain]]
    <br>
- The relationship between variables may be unknown, approximate, or noisy
    <br>
- Scatter plots are preferred to avoid implying a false model
    <br>
- Connecting lines are optional and should not imply causality or linearity
    <br>
- Effects plots are exploratory, not predictive
    

  

# **Examples:**

---

> [!example]
> An engineer measures the output voltage of a temperature sensor while gradually increasing ambient temperature. Plotting voltage versus temperature reveals sensitivity, noise, and approximate linearity, but does not assume the sensor follows an exact transfer function.

  

- Commonly used during calibration and characterization
    
- Useful for identifying trends and non-ideal behavior
    

### Effects plots are used to:

- Identify trends
    
- Estimate sensitivity
    
- Reveal nonlinearity
    
- Characterize noise and repeatability
